Grants for deadlier cigarettes

Sydney Morning Herald
http://www.smh.com.au/news/0002/28/text/pageone1.html



Date: 28/02/2000
By MARGO KINGSTON in Canberra

Taxpayers are paying tobacco companies to develop high-tar, "higher puff count" cigarettes for export to Africa, and to reduce production costs, despite the Government's much-touted national strategy to reduce smoking.

The revelation follows a long struggle by the Australian Medical Association to get information from the Industry Minister, Senator Minchin, on research and development grants, culminating in the Government's refusal to disclose funding levels.

The AMA president, Dr David Brand, said the Government finally responded to a freedom of information request, but blanked out the amount of cash granted.

The documents disclose Philip Morris's research and development tax concession claims for work on the long, high-tar cigarette for Africa, new filters to improve "sensory characteristics and satisfaction", a new machine to make cigarettes more quickly, and a small cigarette called Mighty Mouse.

The revelation exposes the lack of any "public interest test" for R and D funding, or any ban on taxpayer-funded research into anti-social products for export.

The issue is also difficult for Labor, which began the R and D scheme 14 years ago, and the Opposition Leader, Mr Beazley, admitted yesterday that tobacco companies could also get taxpayer funding under Labor.

But he said new policies meant a Labor government would restrict R and D subsidies to "the new industries that we need ... I strongly suspect that cigarettes don't come into that category".

However, Senator Minchin refused to ban funding to tobacco companies and rejected any public interest or morality test on R and D subsidies.

"This is the first time I've heard it suggested that the Government should discriminate against any industry that pays tax," he said. "Everyone is treated equally in the company tax system, and this is a company tax deduction, in effect.

"Governments decide what products can be legally produced and what products can't. At the same time we warn our citizens of the dangers of smoking. These two must and should coexist in a free society. There is no case for discrimination - what other industries would then be subject to this moral approval?"

Asked whether the Government should subsidise research on dangerous products for export to poor countries without rigorous warnings, he said that "there might be a whole range of products which can't be sold in Australia but are designed for export ... that would be an extremely difficult line to draw and policy to apply".

Dr Brand replied that "if someone said now 'We have a product that will kill 18,000 Australians a year and cost the community $12.7 billion a year' there'd be a riot - the Government has just got to start acting on the biggest health problem in this country after indigenous health".

A spokeswoman for the Health Minister, Dr Wooldridge, who negotiated a national anti-tobacco strategy with State health ministers two years ago, said: "It's a decision that's been made in the industry portfolio - the health portfolio is doing its bit to reduce smoking in the community."

The NSW Federal Liberal MP Dr Brendan Nelson called for an immediate funding ban.

"There is no justification at all for giving away taxpayer money for anything other than reducing tobacco consumption. If the Government's got this cash to spend it should spend it on anti-smoking campaigns."

The Professor of Public Health at Sydney University, Professor Simon Chapman, said funding for anti-smoking campaigns had fallen under the Coalition, which had also failed to act on promises to force tobacco companies to tell consumers what was in cigarettes.

"Michael Wooldridge is personally extremely committed, but he's completely outnumbered by the people in Cabinet who say tobacco is like any other product."

A spokesman for Philip Morris said the company got $1.3 million in tax concessions between 1995 and 1998.
